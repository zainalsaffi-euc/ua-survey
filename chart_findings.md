# UA Survey - Chart Findings

## Chart 1 & 2: Weight Loss Status and Access

About two thirds (67.2%) of respondents are not currently on medicated weight loss. Of those who are, the majority (55.8%) access it through a GP or pharmacist, while 44.2% use another telehealth provider. This tells us that traditional healthcare channels are still the primary entry point, but telehealth is not far behind.

## Chart 3: Current Thinking Stage (Non-Users)

Among the 2,588 non-users, nearly half (43.6%) are actively researching medicated weight loss and another 31.5% say they are ready but haven't started yet. Only 4.3% said they are not interested. The vast majority of this audience is already warm and moving toward action.

## Chart 4: Barriers to Starting

Cost and affordability is by far the biggest barrier, both when people could select multiple options and when asked to pick their single biggest one (51.9%). Potential side effects came second, followed by concerns about weight regain after stopping. Fear of starting and judgment from others were the least selected barriers.

## Chart 5: What Matters Most When Considering a Program

Cost (29%) and long-term results (27%) are nearly tied as the top priorities. Trust and clinical credibility came in third at 18.3%, followed by medical and expert support. Community was the least important factor at just 0.4%.

## Chart 6: Juniper Cost vs Value Perception

Almost half of respondents (47.2%) said they are not sure or don't have enough info to judge Juniper's pricing. Among those with an opinion, most found it expensive but understandable (23.9%) or just expensive for what you get (16.5%). Only about 12.5% see it as good value or priced right.

## Chart 7: Financial Situation Change

Close to half (46.8%) of respondents said their financial situation or spending confidence has changed in the past 4 to 6 weeks. When broken down by barrier type, those whose biggest barrier is judgment from others or cost/affordability were the most likely to report a financial change. This suggests that economic pressure is actively shaping people's hesitation.

## Chart 8: Barrier by Thinking Stage Heatmap

Cost and affordability dominates across every thinking stage, but it is most heavily concentrated among those who are ready to start but haven't (59.9%) and those actively researching (52.3%). Side effects as a barrier spikes among the "not interested" group (29.5%), suggesting clinical concerns may be a bigger turnoff for people further from action.

## Chart 9: Audience Funnel

Nearly all respondents (100%) are aware of or have considered medicated weight loss. The biggest drop-off happens between "curious or beyond" (97.1%) and "actively researching or beyond" (83.3%), and again from there to "ready to start or beyond" (54%). Only 32.8% are currently on medication, showing a large pool of potential users stuck in the consideration phase.

## Chart 10: Brand Perception - Juniper vs GP

GPs and pharmacists are perceived more favourably than Juniper on almost every attribute, especially on being medically credible, trustworthy, and judgement-free. Juniper's closest gap to GPs is on "supportive" and "personalised," but it still trails. The biggest perception gaps are on medical credibility and convenience.

## Chart 11: Free-Text Themes - What People Wish Was Explained

Side effects (16.2%) and cost/affordability (14.1%) were the top things people wished were better explained. Long-term effects came third at 10.9%. People are looking for more transparency around what the experience actually looks and feels like, especially the risks and the real costs involved.

## Chart 12: Number of Barriers Selected Per Non-User

Most non-users (45.4%) selected just one barrier, while about a third (33.4%) selected the maximum of three. The average was 1.88 barriers per person. This means a decent portion of the audience is dealing with multiple concerns at once, not just a single blocker.

## Chart 13: Chi-Square Effect Sizes

All three tested relationships were statistically significant. The strongest association was between barrier type and financial change (V=0.173), followed by barrier type and thinking stage (V=0.119), and Juniper value perception and financial change (V=0.110). These are all in the weak to moderate range, meaning the relationships exist but are not overwhelmingly strong.

## Chart 14: Barrier Co-occurrence Matrix

Cost and eligibility concerns are negatively correlated (r = -0.34), meaning people tend to worry about one or the other but rarely both. Side effects and weight regain have a mild positive correlation (r = 0.17), suggesting these clinical concerns tend to cluster together. Most other barrier pairs have little to no relationship.

## Chart 15: Key Proportions with Confidence Intervals

The confidence intervals confirm the reliability of the key metrics. For example, the proportion of non-users actively researching sits between 41.7% and 45.5%, and cost as the biggest barrier falls between 50.0% and 53.8%. The tight intervals across all measures give us strong confidence in the findings given the sample sizes.

## Chart 16: Audience Segments

The largest segment is "Cost-Concerned" at 57.8% of non-users, followed by "Eligibility-Uncertain" (14.9%) and "Clinically-Concerned" (12.8%). When looking at thinking stage by segment, the Cost-Concerned group has the highest share of people who are ready to start but haven't, meaning they are the closest to converting if the price barrier is addressed.

## Chart 17: Word Frequency Lexicon Analysis

In the free-text responses, the most frequent individual words were "weight," "cost," and "loss." The most common phrases were "side effects" and "long-term side effects," which appeared far more than anything else. The language clusters neatly into three themes: cost/affordability concerns, eligibility and medical questions, and worries about weight regain or stopping treatment.
